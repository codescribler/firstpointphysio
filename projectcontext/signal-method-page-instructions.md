# How to Create a Signal Method Page

A standalone document you present to a client (or prospective client) to explain the messaging strategy behind their website copy, why it drives conversions, and why working with Dreamfree is fundamentally different from hiring a typical web designer.

This document does three jobs:

1. **Educates** — shows the client why most websites fail at converting visitors
2. **Differentiates** — positions Dreamfree's process (The Signal Method) as a strategic service, not just design
3. **Converts** — ends with a clear CTA that moves them to the next step

---

## Before You Start

You need two things:

1. **A completed website or demo** — so you can reference specific copy decisions you've already made.
2. **A completed BrandScript** — even a rough one. You need to know the answers to:
   - Who is the customer? (Character)
   - What do they want?
   - What problem stops them? How does it make them feel?
   - How does the brand show empathy and authority? (Guide)
   - What's the simple 3-step plan?
   - What's the call to action?
   - What happens if they don't act? (Failure)
   - What does success look like?

If you haven't formally written a BrandScript, pull it from the website copy — the answers are embedded in the decisions you already made.

---

## Page Structure

The document has 8 sections. Each one builds on the last — don't reorder them.

---

### Cover Page

- Badge: "Supplementary — Website Proposal" (or "Messaging Strategy" or "Appendix")
- Title: "The Messaging Strategy Behind Your New Website"
- Subtitle: One sentence explaining the framework was used to drive [the client's goal — bookings, enquiries, sales, etc.]
- Meta: Client name, Dreamfree / Daniel Whittaker, no date needed (it's supplementary to the main proposal)

**Adapt the subtitle to the client's conversion goal.** For a physio clinic it might be "helps patients decide to book an appointment." For an accountant it might be "helps business owners pick up the phone."

---

### Section 1: The Problem With Most Business Websites

**Purpose:** Name the elephant in the room before introducing the solution. This sets up the entire document by making the client feel understood — and making them realise the problem is bigger than they thought.

**Structure:**
- Opening paragraph: Most businesses struggle with website copy. They know what they do, but when it comes to writing it down in a way that resonates with customers, they're stuck. The result? Websites that talk about the business instead of talking to the customer.
- Second paragraph: This isn't their fault. Nobody teaches business owners how to write website copy. So they default to what they've seen — "Welcome to [business name]", a wall of text about their history, and a contact page buried three clicks deep. It looks professional. It says nothing.
- Third paragraph: The consequence — visitors leave. Not because the business isn't good, but because the website didn't give them a reason to stay. Confused people don't convert. They click away.
- Closing line: "This is why we don't just design your website — we write the copy too. And we do it using a proven framework."

**Tone:** Empathetic, not condescending. The client should feel "that's exactly what happened to me" — not "I'm being told off." You're the guide acknowledging their problem, not a lecturer.

**Keep it tight.** Three or four paragraphs max. This section creates the tension that the rest of the document resolves.

---

### Section 2: What Is The Signal Method?

**Purpose:** Introduce your methodology. Position it as a refined, proprietary approach built on established foundations.

**Structure:**
- Opening paragraph: Introduce The Signal Method as Dreamfree's approach to writing website copy that converts. It's built on the StoryBrand framework — a messaging methodology created by Donald Miller, used by thousands of businesses worldwide. The core insight: "If you confuse, you lose."
- Second paragraph: Explain the principle — storytelling structure applied to marketing, where the customer is the hero and the business is the guide. Most websites make the business the hero. That's backwards.
- A Donald Miller quote (use the blockquote style):

> "People don't buy the best products. They buy the products they can understand the fastest."
> — Donald Miller, Building a StoryBrand

- Third paragraph: Explain what makes The Signal Method different from just "using StoryBrand." This is where you describe the Dreamfree process:

**The Signal Method Process:**
1. **Interview** — We sit down with you and ask the right questions about your business, your customers, and what makes you different. This is a structured conversation, not a questionnaire.
2. **Transcribe & Research** — We transcribe the interview and combine it with market research, competitor analysis, and customer insight to build a complete picture.
3. **BrandScript** — We distil everything into a clear messaging framework — your BrandScript — that defines exactly what your website needs to say and why.
4. **Copy & Build** — We write every word of your website copy based on the BrandScript, then design and build the site around that message.

- Closing line: "You don't need to write a single word. We handle it — because most businesses shouldn't be writing their own website copy. Not because they can't write, but because they're too close to their own business to see it the way a customer does."

**Key positioning note:** This section should make clear that you're not just a web designer who makes things look nice. You're a strategist who figures out the right message first and builds the website around it. That's the difference. That's what justifies premium pricing. The process IS the product.

---

### Section 3: The Seven-Part Story

**Purpose:** Show the SB7 framework and map each element to a specific decision you made on their site.

**Structure:** Seven numbered steps, each with:
- **Label** — the framework element (A Character, Has a Problem, Meets a Guide, etc.)
- **Title** — what this means for their specific business (use their actual copy or a summary of it)
- **Description** — one or two sentences explaining the decision

**How to fill in each step for a new client:**

| Step | Framework Element | What to Write |
|------|------------------|---------------|
| 1 | A Character | Describe their customer in one phrase. "The homeowner looking for a builder." "The patient in pain who just wants to move freely again." |
| 2 | Has a Problem | Pull the problem statement from your website copy. Quote it directly if possible. Name the emotional problem, not just the practical one. |
| 3 | Meets a Guide | The client's business. Explain how the copy shows both empathy ("we understand") and authority (stats, experience, qualifications, specifics). |
| 4 | Who Gives Them a Plan | State the 3-step plan you used on the site. Quote the exact steps. |
| 5 | And Calls Them to Action | Name the CTA text and where it appears on the site. |
| 6 | That Helps Them Avoid Failure | What goes wrong if they don't act? What anxiety does the site address? |
| 7 | And Ends in Success | Quote the success vision from the copy. What does life look like after? |

**Tip:** Use the client's actual headlines and copy in the titles. "I just want to get back to playing with my kids without wincing" is more powerful than "We addressed the customer's internal problem."

---

### Section 4: How This Shapes the Website Copy

**Purpose:** A practical table showing "we did X because of Y."

**Structure:** Two-column table.
- Column 1: **Signal Method Principle** (e.g., "Customer is the hero", "Name the problem", "Repeat the CTA")
- Column 2: **What We Did on the Site** — specific, concrete examples from their actual pages

**Aim for 6-8 rows.** The standard principles to cover:

1. Customer is the hero (headline focuses on them, not the business)
2. Name the problem (empathy section on the homepage)
3. Show empathy + authority (tone + specific proof points)
4. Give them a plan (the 3-step process)
5. Repeat the CTA (where the button appears)
6. Remove friction (FAQs, upfront information, transparent pricing)
7. Paint success (the aspirational outcome in the copy)
8. Transparent pricing (if applicable — stating cost early and clearly)

**Pull direct quotes from the site.** Don't paraphrase — show the actual words. This makes it concrete and verifiable.

---

### Section 5: Why This Helps [Clients' Customers] [Convert]

**Purpose:** The psychology section. Explains why the approach works, not just what it is.

**This section has three parts:**

**Part A — Four feature cards explaining the principles:**

Use this set and adapt the descriptions:

1. **Clarity in 5 Seconds** — the Grunt Test. Could someone understand the site in 5 seconds?
2. **Reduces Mental Effort** — confused visitors leave. Story structure keeps them engaged.
3. **Answers the Real Questions** — emotional questions ("will this work for me?"), not just practical ones.
4. **Always a Clear Next Step** — no dead ends. Every section leads somewhere.

These four are universal — they work for any client. Just adjust the description examples to match their industry.

**Part B — The Visitor Journey Table:**

This is the most powerful part of the page. Two columns:
- **What They See** — the actual section/headline on the site
- **What They Think** — the internal monologue of the visitor

Walk through the homepage top to bottom. 6-8 rows. Write the "What They Think" column in first person, italicised.

**How to write this for a new client:**
1. Open their homepage
2. Scroll from top to bottom
3. For each major section, ask: "What would a visitor think at this point?"
4. Write it as their internal voice

**Example for a physiotherapy client:**

| What They See | What They Think |
|---|---|
| Hero: "Get back to doing what you love — without the pain" | *"That's exactly what I want. They get it."* |
| "Living with pain affects everything — your sleep, your mood, your confidence" | *"Yes. It's not just physical. Someone understands."* |
| 3 steps: Book → Assess → Recover | *"Simple. I don't have to figure anything out."* |
| "15 years of experience. 3,000+ patients treated." | *"They know what they're doing."* |
| Testimonial: "I can play football with my son again" | *"That's what I want. Real results."* |
| "Book Your Appointment" | *"No risk. I'll do it."* |

**Example for a builder:**

| What They See | What They Think |
|---|---|
| Hero: "Your dream kitchen, delivered on time" | *"OK, they do kitchens. On time is important to me."* |
| "Renovations go wrong when communication breaks down" | *"That's exactly what happened last time."* |
| 3 steps: Call → Plan → Enjoy | *"Sounds straightforward. Not complicated."* |
| Testimonials with photos | *"Real people. They look happy with the result."* |
| "Get a Free Quote" | *"No commitment. I'll fill this in."* |

**Part C — The green highlight box:**

A single-paragraph summary: "People don't [convert] because of features. They [convert] because the website made them feel confident that [the outcome] and that there won't be surprises."

Adapt the verb (book, call, enquire, buy) and the outcome to the client.

---

### Section 6: Generic Copy vs. Signal Method Copy

**Purpose:** A side-by-side comparison that makes the difference visually obvious.

**Structure:** Two cards — "Before" (grey, X marks) and "After" (green, checkmarks).

**The left card (Typical [Industry] Website):**
- Leads with "Welcome to [business name]"
- Opens with history/about the founder
- Features listed without context
- One small contact link
- Visitor has to work out if it's suitable
- No clear path from interest to action

**The right card (The Signal Method):**
- Leads with what the customer wants
- Opens with the customer's problem
- Benefits framed around their situation
- CTA visible immediately and repeated
- Site answers their questions proactively
- Guided journey to [conversion action]

**Adapt the left column to the client's industry.** Use language that reflects what their competitors' websites actually look like. The more recognisable it is, the stronger the contrast. Browse their competitors before writing this section — specificity sells.

**Closing line:** "Both websites describe the same [business/venue/service]. The difference is that the Signal Method version is structured to help someone *decide*, not just *browse*."

---

### Section 7: In Summary

**Purpose:** Tie it together in a few lines. Reinforce the main point.

**Structure:**
- Opening line: "The proposed website isn't just a visual upgrade..."
- A highlight box with a bulleted list of 5-6 things the framework ensures (customer focus, acknowledges the problem, removes doubt, simple path, repeats CTA, paints success)
- Reinforce the differentiator: "Every word on the site was written with purpose — informed by a structured interview, real research, and a proven messaging framework. This isn't template copy. It's strategy."
- Closing line: "The result is a website that doesn't just look good — it *works*."

---

### Section 8: What Happens Next (CTA)

**Purpose:** Close the document with a clear, low-friction next step. Don't let the document end passively — it should move them to act.

**Structure:**
- A section header: "Ready to Talk About Your Website?"
- One or two paragraphs that feel like a natural next step, not a hard sell. Reinforce that the first step is a conversation — you'll listen, understand their business, and explain how The Signal Method would apply to them specifically.
- Emphasise what they get from the conversation: clarity on what's wrong with their current site, an understanding of what good looks like, and a feel for whether working together is the right fit.
- A prominent CTA button or highlight box: **"Get in touch to discuss your website"**
- Contact details: email, phone number (if appropriate), and/or a link to book a call
- Optional — a single reassuring line: "No commitment, no pressure. Just a conversation about how your website could work harder for your business."

**Tone:** Warm, confident, consultative. You're inviting them into a conversation, not closing a sale. Think guide, not salesperson. The document has already done the selling — this section just opens the door.

**Positioning note:** Do NOT undercut yourself here. No "free audit" or "no-obligation quote" language that signals low value. A conversation with you IS the valuable thing. Frame it that way.

## Design & Styling

The page uses the same styling as the main proposal document. Key elements:

- **Fonts:** Playfair Display (headings) + Inter (body)
- **Colours:** Primary red `#C41E2A`, warm cream background `#FAF7F2`, green for positive `#1B7340`
- **Section headers:** Red numbered circle + heading
- **Alternating backgrounds:** White and warm cream (`page--warm`)
- **Components used:** highlight boxes, feature cards (2-col grid), tables, before/after cards, blockquotes, framework steps, CTA box
- **Print-friendly:** Page breaks between sections, no shadows on print

Copy the `<style>` block from the original `storybrand-approach.html` as the starting point. The CSS is self-contained — no external stylesheet needed.

## Checklist Before Sending

- [ ] Cover subtitle matches the client's conversion goal (book, call, enquire, buy)
- [ ] Section 1 resonates with their specific situation — adapt examples to their industry
- [ ] Section 2 process steps feel natural — adjust if the engagement didn't follow the exact 4-step flow
- [ ] Section 3 uses the client's actual copy in the step titles, not generic descriptions
- [ ] Section 4 table references real pages and real headlines from their site
- [ ] Section 5 visitor journey table walks through their actual homepage, top to bottom
- [ ] Section 6 left card reflects what their competitors' websites actually look like
- [ ] The word "book" / "enquire" / "buy" is correct throughout (find and replace)
- [ ] Client name and business name are correct in the cover and footer
- [ ] Section 8 CTA has correct contact details and feels natural, not salesy
- [ ] No references to other clients left in the copy
- [ ] Print preview looks clean — no orphaned headings or split tables

## Quick-Start: Copy and Adapt

1. Duplicate the base Signal Method HTML template
2. Rename to `signal-method-[client].html`
3. Find and replace: client name throughout
4. Find and replace: "book" / "booking" → their conversion verb if different
5. Adapt Section 1 examples to their industry
6. Rewrite Section 3 step titles using their BrandScript
7. Rewrite Section 4 table with their specific copy decisions
8. Rewrite Section 5 visitor journey using their homepage
9. Adjust Section 6 left card for their industry
10. Update Section 8 CTA with relevant contact details
11. Update cover, footer, and summary
12. Print preview and check
